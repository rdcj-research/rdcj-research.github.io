Methods & Principles

Working orientation

RDCJ Research operates as a laboratory rather than a publication pipeline. The work begins with questions, but it does not assume that the quickest route to an answer is the most truthful one. Inquiry is treated as a structural practice: building conceptual scaffolds, testing mappings across domains, and allowing formalisms to emerge when they can be justified rather than when they are demanded.

The aim is not to replace established scientific or mathematical methods, but to examine the conditions under which different methods become valid, useful, or misleading, especially in domains where meaning, interpretation, and human cognition are part of the system being studied.

Principles of inquiry

  1. Questions before conclusions
    Questions are treated as primary research objects. A good question can outlive a correct answer; it can be refined, decomposed, or reframed without losing its identity.
  2. Structural comparison across domains
    Many investigations proceed through comparison: identifying recurring structures across mathematics, computation, language, music, geometry, and cultural symbolic systems. Similarity is not assumed to be equivalence, it is used as a prompt for careful mapping.
  3. Minimal commitments, explicit assumptions
    Where possible, the work avoids premature metaphysical claims. When assumptions are required (ontological, epistemic, computational), they are stated explicitly and revised when they fail.
  4. Anti-reduction without anti-rigor
    The laboratory is cautious of reductionism in domains where meaning and context are constitutive. This is not a rejection of formalisation, it is a refusal to treat formalisation as the only legitimate mode of knowledge.
  5. Form follows evidence
    The output form is chosen based on what the inquiry produces: sometimes a paper, sometimes a catalogue, sometimes an artefact. A result is considered valid when it becomes communicable, testable (where appropriate), and structurally coherent.

Research workflow

Capture → Structure → Test → Publish → Iterate

  • Capture: Notes, fragments, diagrams, prompts, reading trails, and anomalies are collected without forcing early coherence.
  • Structure: Candidate structures are extracted (definitions, relationships, transformations, symmetries, constraints).
  • Test: Structures are checked through:
    • internal consistency (does the framing contradict itself?)
    • cross-domain mapping (does it preserve meaning across contexts?)
    • constructive pressure (can it generate new questions, models, or artefacts?)
  • Publish: Outputs are released as:
    • papers (claims, arguments, definitions)
    • catalogues (structured collections and mappings)
    • artefacts (music, interfaces, physical devices)
  • Iterate: Publications are treated as provisional. They may be refined, split into smaller pieces, or recombined into a later synthesis.

Evidence and validation

Validation depends on the category of work.

  • Formal / mathematical work >is validated through definitional clarity, derivation, and proof-like constraint satisfaction.
  • Computational work is validated through implementability, reproducibility, and behavioural clarity.
  • Epistemic and interpretive work is validated through coherence, comparative explanatory power, and the ability to generate non-trivial distinctions.
  • Artefacts are validated as operational interfaces: they must make a structure perceptible, interactable, and meaningfully constrained, not merely aesthetic.

Across all types, the main failure mode avoided is premature certainty.

Publication posture

RDCJ Research publishes in public, but does not optimise for reach. The default stance is slow release: fewer pieces, higher coherence, and clear versioning.

Drafts may be visible. Some work will remain incomplete. This is not treated as a flaw, but as an accurate reflection of inquiry as it unfolds over time.

Boundaries

The laboratory maintains boundaries to preserve clarity:

  • It does not present speculation as settled fact.
  • It does not treat symbolic systems as supernatural claims; they are studied as embedded logics and cognitive technologies.
  • It does not attempt to “solve everything” through one framework.
  • It prioritises depth over coverage and structure over rhetoric.
