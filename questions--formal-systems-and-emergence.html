Formal Systems & Emergence

How does emergent structure arise within formal systems, and what roles do resonance, constraint, and geometry play in this process?

This line of inquiry investigates emergence as a structural phenomenon rather than an anomaly. It focuses on how complexity arises from constrained systems, and how mathematical, geometric, and combinatorial structures give rise to unexpected order.

A Wave–Matrix Ontology: Normalisation, Geometry Without Axes, and the Resolution of Mathematical Absolutes

Modern mathematics and physics rely heavily on scalar quantities, fixed constants, and coordinate-based geometry to describe the natural world. While these tools have proven extraordinarily effective, they introduce persistent conceptual tensions: the ontological status of constants, the privileged role of numbers, the dependence on coordinate systems, and the difficulty of reconciling asymmetry, dynamism, and relationality with absolute formulations.

Music as a Native Wave–Modal System

This paper demonstrates that music constitutes a native instantiation of a Wave–Modal Ontology (WMO), rather than an analogy or illustrative application layered upon it. We show that musical identity does not depend on absolute frequency (hertz), linear time, or an external observer, but instead arises from divisions of an undifferentiated oscillatory field, weighted through accentuation under spatial and energetic constraints. Melody, harmony, and perceived motion emerge from the redistribution and dissipation of energy within this field, with time appearing only as an indexing construct rather than a fundamental primitive. By analysing music as a complete, operational, and embodied system, this paper provides a concrete validation of WMO’s core claims: scale invariance, observer-free collapse, space-first ontology, and persistence of identity under deformation. Music is therefore shown not as a metaphor for WMO, but as one of its most accessible and mature realizations.

Entropy under Wave–Modal Ontology

Irreversibility without time, disorder, or probability

This paper reformulates entropy within the Wave–Modal Ontology (WMO) as a structural consequence of energy dispersion in an oscillatory field, rather than as disorder, ignorance, or probabilistic uncertainty. We show that irreversibility arises from the exhaustion of symmetric configurations under constraint, without requiring fundamental time, statistical ensembles, or observer-dependent measures. Entropy is reinterpreted as the progressive loss of available modal coherence, aligning thermodynamics with the space-first, observer-free framework established in Papers 1 and 1.5. This formulation preserves compatibility with classical and statistical thermodynamics while removing their ontological dependence on time and probability as primitives.

Symbolic Algebra and Computation under Wave–Modal Ontology

Computation without constants

This paper demonstrates that the Wave–Modal Ontology (WMO) admits symbolic representation and computation without reliance on absolute constants, fixed scalars, or primitive equality. We introduce a minimal symbolic algebra and illustrative domain-specific language (DSL) whose sole purpose is to show that WMO is compatible with formal computation. Symbols are treated as projections of relational wave–modal structures, and operations are defined in terms of equivalence, transformation, and constraint rather than numeric evaluation. This paper introduces no new ontology; it serves strictly as a legitimacy proof that WMO can be implemented, reasoned with, and executed within formal systems while preserving its core commitments: scale invariance, observer-free dynamics, and non-primitive time.

Primes as a Contrasting Case under Wave–Modal Ontology

When discreteness appears fundamental

This paper examines prime numbers as a contrasting case within the Wave–Modal Ontology (WMO), not with the aim of resolving open problems in number theory, but to clarify why certain discrete mathematical phenomena appear ontologically fundamental under projection-based frameworks. We argue that primes arise as artefacts of discrete symbolic projection imposed upon an underlying continuous, relational structure. Within WMO, indivisibility is not a primitive property of reality, but a consequence of representational constraints that privilege integer decomposition. By reframing primes as structural features of projection rather than as irreducible ontological objects, this paper illustrates how WMO dissolves the appearance of deep anomaly without replacing or competing with classical number theory.

Ontological Commitments and Near-Miss Realisms: Situating the Wave–Modal Ontology

This paper situates the Wave–Modal Ontology (WMO) within the landscape of established ontological positions by examining the commitments they make regarding substance, process, structure, and realism. Rather than extending or defending WMO, the analysis clarifies where WMO aligns with, diverges from, or deliberately withholds commitments common to these frameworks. Particular attention is given to “near-miss realisms”—positions that share surface similarities with WMO yet differ decisively in their treatment of mathematical structures and dimensionality. The goal is to provide conceptual orientation: to make clear what kind of ontology WMO is, and what it intentionally refuses to be.

Physical Formalisms as Projections: Interpreting Classical, Relativistic, and Entropic Frameworks under WMO

This paper examines how major physical formalisms—classical mechanics, thermodynamics, special relativity, and information-theoretic framings—may be interpreted under the Wave–Modal Ontology (WMO) without modification to their empirical content. Rather than proposing alternatives to these theories, the analysis treats them as successful representational projections arising under specific constraints. By reframing time, state, and entropy as emergent indexing and structural asymmetries rather than primitives, WMO offers a unifying interpretive layer that clarifies why these formalisms work, where their limits lie, and how their apparent tensions may be understood as artefacts of representational choice.

Representation, Discreteness, and Symbolic Collapse: Mathematics and Computation under WMO

This paper examines how mathematical objects, discrete symbols, and computational formalisms arise under the Wave–Modal Ontology (WMO). Rather than treating numbers, symbols, or computational states as ontologically primitive, WMO interprets them as outcomes of symbolic collapse: the projection of constrained oscillatory relations into stable, countable representations. By situating mathematics and computation downstream of constraint and projection, this analysis clarifies why discrete formalisms are effective, where their limits lie, and how their apparent fundamentality can be understood without reifying abstraction.
